Introduction

Interoperability of devices and services for the Internet of Things with cloud platforms and services at a worldwide scale is a huge challenge.

Recent developments in electronics have dramatically reduced the cost for devices functioning as tags, sensors and actuators for the physical environment, i.e. the Internet of Things (IoT). The market potential for IoT devices and services is currently held back by fragmentation due to a plethora of communication technologies and the lack of a common approach to enabling services.

RESTful Web technologies have become very important in recent years, such as JavaScript+JSON combined with open standards for data formats, interface definitions and access control.

The mission of the Web of Things activities is to counter the fragmentation of the Internet of Things by introducing a Web-based abstraction layer capable of interconnecting existing Internet of Things platforms, devices and cloud services and complementing available standards. Baseline specifications have been developed by the Web of Things groups during the last two years to address an architecture and a common format for describing devices and services.

There is a very wide range of application domains, and the following is just a sample:

  • Smart Home
  • Security for homes, businesses and public settings
  • Smart Cities
  • Environmental monitoring
  • Healthcare at home and in hospitals
  • Manufacturing, construction and retail
  • Transportation: cars, buses, metro, trains
  • Utilities: electricity, water, gas, drainage
  • Energy efficiency, smart appliances and the smart grid
  • Managing emergencies: floods, fires, earthquakes and civil disturbances
To achieve the goal for cross-platform interoperability, the W3C Web of Things Working Group has finalized a first set of standardized building blocks. They are based upon rich metadata that describes the data and interaction models exposed to applications, and the communications and security requirements for platforms to communicate effectively. During the course of the Web of Things standardisation activities, the following specifications were created:
  • WoT Architecture
  • WoT Thing Description with its Properties, Actions, Events interaction model.
  • WoT Scripting API
  • WoT Binding Templates"
  • Security and Privacy Considerations

A W3C Workshop is now planned for early 2019 to examine the potential for further development of these open standards as a basis for interoperable services between devices, at the network edge, or in the cloud. It will discuss reducing of ftagmentation through the use of common device abstractions, web protocols and scripting languages for exposing and consuming services across specific IoT technologies to enable new use cases, such as a common monitoring and control of assets from multiple vendors for building a digital twin of a group of physical devices.

See this Workshop's Call for Participation. Further background is given below.

Who Should Attend?

  • Organizations interested in realizing use cases for the Web of Things
  • Web developers with an experience of creating IoT platforms, applications and services
  • Device vendors seeking to expand their market potential
  • Network operators with an interest in M2M and scalable communications services
  • Cloud platform and application vendors interested in hosting services for the Web of Things
  • System integrators and consultancies with an interest in helping their clients with designing and deploying applications and services based upon the Web of Things
  • Governments and regulatory agencies interested in enabling open markets for the Web of Things, and the associated challenges for security and privacy

Workshop Topics

  • Business models, scenarios, and use cases
  • Concrete scenarios for consumer, industrial, environmental, energy management and smart cities applications
  • Application ecosystem enabling, including application marketplaces
  • IoT semantics and information models
  • Cloud applications, platforms and services
  • Edge computing integration
  • Digital twins
  • Interoperability, testing, and validation
  • Device capabilities and profiles
  • Extension management and validation
  • Device and lifecycle management
  • Security and privacy
  • Tools and applications
  • Directories, proxies, discovery mechanisms, and other infrastructure services
  • Scripting and other orchestration mechanisms
  • Hypermedia patterns for actions and events
  • Streaming media and data
  • Thing description profiles
  • Enabling advanced user interfaces, including speech
  • Browser and web application integration
  • (MAYBE) Time synchronization

What is W3C?

W3C is a voluntary standards consortium that convenes companies and communities to help structure productive discussions around existing and emerging technologies, and offers a Royalty-Free patent framework for Web Recommendations. W3C develops work based on the priorities of our members and our community.
